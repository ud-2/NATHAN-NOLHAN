### SECTION 1: BIOLOGY - Cells and Organisms (Unit 1)

**Question 1: Comparing Plant and Animal Cells (5 marks)**  
A scientist observes a specimen under a high-powered electron microscope and draws a model of the cell. She notes the presence of a nucleus, mitochondria, a cell membrane, and cytoplasm, but she cannot find any chloroplasts, cell wall, or a large permanent vacuole.
* a) Deduce whether this is a plant cell or an animal cell, providing **two** pieces of evidence from her observations to justify your answer. [2]
* b) The scientist observes that the cell has an unusually high number of mitochondria. Explain what this suggests about the function and activity level of this specific cell. [2]
* c) State the specific function of the cell membrane. [1]

**Question 2: Specialised Cells (5 marks)**  
Organisms rely on specialised cells to survive. Look at the structure of a red blood cell and a root hair cell.
* a) A red blood cell does not contain a nucleus. Explain how this unique adaptation helps it perform its specific function in the human body. [2]
* b) Explain how the specific shape of a root hair cell makes it highly efficient at its job. [2]
* c) Name **one** other specialised animal cell and state its function. [1]

**Question 3: From Cells to Organisms (5 marks)**  
Complex multicellular organisms are highly organised.
* a) Define the biological term *tissue*. [2]
* b) The heart is a vital organ. Name **two** different types of tissue you would expect to find in the heart. [2]
* c) Place the following structures in order of size, starting from the smallest: *Respiratory System, Epithelial Cell, Human, Lung, Lung Tissue*. [1]

**Question 4: Models and Representations in Biology (5 marks)**  
A student makes a 3D model of a plant cell using a clear plastic box filled with jelly. He uses a green grape to represent the nucleus and small green peas to represent chloroplasts.
* a) Evaluate his model: state **one** strength of using the plastic box and jelly. [1]
* b) Identify **one** scientific inaccuracy in his model regarding the nucleus. [1]
* c) Which actual plant cell structure is missing from his model that would normally surround the jelly (cytoplasm) but be inside the plastic box (cell wall)? [1]
* d) Why do scientists use models to represent cells? [2]

### SECTION 2: CHEMISTRY - Materials, States, and the Water Cycle (Unit 2.1 - 2.4)

**Question 5: Solids, Liquids, and Gases (5 marks)**  
A student uses a syringe. She pulls the plunger back to fill it with air, seals the end with her finger, and pushes the plunger in. She repeats the experiment, but fills the syringe with liquid water instead.
* a) She finds that she can compress the air, but she cannot compress the water. Explain these observations using the particle theory of matter. [3]
* b) Describe the arrangement of particles in a solid compared to a liquid. [2]

**Question 6: Explaining Changes of State (5 marks)**  
A block of solid gallium metal melts in a person's hand at $29.7^\circ\text{C}$.
* a) State the scientific term for the process of a solid turning into a liquid. [1]
* b) Describe what happens to the energy and movement of the gallium particles as heat from the hand transfers to the metal block. [2]
* c) If the liquid gallium is placed in a freezer, it will turn back into a solid. Does the mass of the gallium change during this process? Explain your answer. [2]

**Question 7: The Water Cycle (5 marks)**  
The water cycle describes how water moves on, above, and below the surface of the Earth.
* a) Explain the role of the Sun's thermal energy in the water cycle. Use the correct scientific term for the change of state involved. [2]
* b) Describe the process of condensation in the atmosphere and state what weather phenomenon it creates. [2]
* c) Water often contains dissolved impurities. Explain why rainwater is generally pure water when it falls from the clouds. [1]

**Question 8: Investigating Evaporation (5 marks)**  
A student sets up an experiment to investigate how temperature affects the rate of evaporation. He places $50\text{ ml}$ of water into three identical beakers and leaves them at $10^\circ\text{C}$, $20^\circ\text{C}$, and $30^\circ\text{C}$ for 24 hours.
* a) Identify the **independent variable** and the **dependent variable** in this investigation. [2]
* b) State **one** variable the student correctly controlled in this experiment. [1]
* c) Predict the results of the experiment using the particle theory to explain your reasoning. [2]

### SECTION 3: CHEMISTRY - Atoms, Elements, and Compounds (Unit 2.5 - 2.7)

**Question 9: Atoms, Elements, and the Periodic Table (5 marks)**  
* a) Define the term *element*. [1]
* b) Describe how the elements are arranged in the Periodic Table. Mention groups and periods in your answer. [2]
* c) A scientist discovers a new substance. She tests it and finds it cannot be broken down into any simpler substances by chemical reactions. Determine whether this substance is an element, compound, or mixture, and justify your answer. [2]

The chemical formula for a molecule of glucose is $C_6H_{12}O_6$.
* a) Explain the difference between an element and a compound. [2]
* b) Using the formula $C_6H_{12}O_6$, state exactly how many atoms of **Carbon** and **Oxygen** are in one molecule of glucose. [1]

**Question 11: Compounds and Mixtures (5 marks)**

* b) The teacher then heats the mixture strongly until it glows red. A new, black, non-magnetic solid is formed called iron sulfide. State **two** ways we know a chemical reaction has taken place. [2]

**Question 12: Chemical Reactions and Mass (5 marks)**  
A student places $10\text{g}$ of baking soda into $50\text{g}$ of vinegar inside a flask. The mixture fizzes violently. After the fizzing stops, the student weighs the flask's contents and finds the mass is now $58\text{g}$.
* a) State the Law of Conservation of Mass. [2]
* b) Explain why the final mass ($58\text{g}$) is less than the starting mass ($10\text{g} + 50\text{g} = 60\text{g}$), and explain how the student could change the experiment to prove the Law of Conservation of Mass is still true. [3]

### SECTION 4: PHYSICS - Forces and Energy (Unit 3)
